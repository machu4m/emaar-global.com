Yes — we offer UAE-court-accepted remote notarization through secure video channels. This is particularly useful for POAs, MOAs, and board resolutions where the principal is overseas. Remote notarization is legally valid in the UAE and internationally in most cases. If you already have your document drafted, remote notarization can often be completed within 1–2 business days.
In most cases, a UAE residency visa and Emirates ID are required for corporate bank account opening. However, we work with partner banks that offer flexible onboarding for certain free zone structures — particularly for e-commerce and consulting businesses where the founder is abroad. Speak to us for a case-by-case assessment. We'll tell you honestly what's possible before you waste time pursuing an unlikely application.
If an application we submit on your behalf gets rejected by any authority, we revise the documentation and resubmit at no additional cost. We minimize rejection risk through accurate preparation — but if it happens, we take responsibility and fix it. We also provide a clear explanation of why the rejection occurred and what changed, so you understand the process.
Yes — most UAE government departments require Arabic versions of contracts, MOAs, power of attorneys, and official applications. Our translators are Ministry of Justice (MOJ) certified, which means their translations are accepted by all UAE courts and government authorities without question. Non-certified translations are frequently rejected, causing costly delays.
For UAE-issued documents, MOFA (Ministry of Foreign Affairs) attestation typically takes 1–3 working days through our internal PRO team. For foreign documents requiring embassy or home-country attestation before MOFA, timelines depend on the specific embassy — this can range from 2 days to 3 weeks. We assess your documents upfront and give you a realistic timeline before you proceed.
DIFC Wills are available only to non-Muslims and operate under common law principles — they are the most popular choice for expats as they closely mirror Western-style estate planning. Dubai Court Wills also exist for non-Muslims but follow UAE civil law. The key differences are in how assets are distributed, how guardianship works, and which courts have jurisdiction. We advise based on your specific asset profile and family situation.
